The Story of the Bear and the Crane

A proposal to make this logo a symbol of our PG Chito-Ryu dojo.

Neither the bear nor the crane are symbols of Chito-Ryu Karate. They do not represent the style as a whole, nor are they drawn from its historical origins. They are symbols of this dojo — and of the qualities we pursue in our practice and in our lives.

The Crane

The Crane perches at the crown of the image, wings spread wide against a red rising sun — watchful, poised, and still. It connects naturally to the technical traditions that flow through Chito-Ryu's lineage. The crane doesn't overpower. It prevails through positioning, awareness, and efficiency.

Precision over force. Timing over speed. Balance under pressure. These are the principles at the heart of Chito-Ryu: using the body correctly, moving with purpose, and not relying on brute strength when skill will do.

The Bear

The Bear anchors the centre — roaring, grounded, claws wrapped around two bo staff. It is a symbol of character more than technique. Of rooted stance and structural strength. Of resilience, protection, and the courage to act when action is called for.

The bear is Northern BC. It is the land we train in and the quiet toughness this region tends to build in people. Our dojo works to cultivate that same quality: strong roots, a calm presence, and decisiveness when it matters.

The Chito-Ryu crest sits at the heart of the image, held between them — a reminder that both qualities serve the same practice.

The crane rises above. The bear holds the ground. Neither outranks the other. Together they represent what we're working toward: the refinement to move well, and the strength to stand firm.
